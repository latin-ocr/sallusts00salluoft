XXI. ¹ Quid ubique] Sallust not only here, but in several other
places, puts ubique for et ubi. Cap. xxvii„ Quem ubique opportunum
credebat, i. e. quem et ubi illum opportunam credebat. Jug. cap. lii.,
Neque remittit, quid ubique bostis ageret, explorare, i. e. quid, et ubi
ageret.—M.

Quid ubique] Add to the examples adduced by Mair, Ter. Hecyr.
4, 2, 3 ; and ib. 28, Si caetera sunt ita, ut tu vis, itaque for que ita3
and Liv. lib. 3, c. 30, Itaque cautum est. So, neque for et ne; see
Jug. c. 14, note—C.

² Tabulas novas] Intellige tabulas debiti et crediti, quae nova di-
cuntur, quando debitoribus aes alienum remittitur.—M.

³ Proscriptionem.] Proscription, first practised by Sulla, was a
posting up persons’ names in public places, with a promise of a certain
reward to any who should murder them. And the estates and goods
of the persons thus proscribed were confiscated and seized.—-M.
